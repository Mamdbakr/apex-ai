# Periodization in Strength and Conditioning

Periodization is the systematic planning of athletic training to reach peak
performance at the right time and avoid overtraining.

## Types of Periodization

### Linear Periodization
- Progressive increase in intensity, decrease in volume over time
- Classic model: weeks 1-4 hypertrophy (high volume), weeks 5-8 strength
  (moderate volume), weeks 9-12 power (low volume, high intensity)
- Best for: beginners and intermediate athletes
- Limitation: adaptation plateaus after several cycles

### Undulating Periodization (DUP)
- Daily or weekly variation in rep ranges and loading
- Example: Monday 4x12 (hypertrophy), Wednesday 4x6 (strength), Friday 4x20 (endurance)
- Provides more frequent variation stimulus
- Better for intermediate and advanced athletes
- Research shows superior hypertrophy gains vs linear periodization

### Block Periodization
- Concentrated training blocks (3-4 weeks) with single dominant quality
- Accumulation → Transmutation → Realization blocks
- Used by elite athletes in annual planning

## Key Concepts

### Volume-Intensity Relationship
- High volume + low intensity = hypertrophy and endurance adaptations
- Low volume + high intensity = strength and power adaptations
- Both variables cannot be maximized simultaneously

### Mesocycles, Microcycles, Macrocycles
- Microcycle: 1 week of training
- Mesocycle: 4-6 week training block
- Macrocycle: full training year

### Deload Weeks
- Scheduled reduction in volume/intensity every 4-8 weeks
- Allows supercompensation and reduces injury risk
- Typical deload: 50-60% of normal volume at same intensity
- Signs that deload is needed: persistent fatigue, declining performance,
  joint pain, poor sleep, decreased motivation

## Practical Application for Hypertrophy
- Weeks 1-4: 3x10-12 per exercise (accumulation)
- Weeks 5-8: 4x6-8 (intensification)
- Week 9: Deload 2x10 at 60% weight
- Repeat with slightly higher weights

## Evidence Base
Based on NSCA Essentials of Strength and Conditioning and meta-analyses
by Schoenfeld (2010), Krieger (2010), and Rhea et al. (2003).
