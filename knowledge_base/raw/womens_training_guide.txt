# Women-Specific Fitness: Training and Nutrition

Women are not smaller men. Key physiological differences require
adjusted training and nutrition approaches.

## Hormonal Considerations

### Menstrual Cycle and Performance
- Follicular phase (days 1-14): Rising estrogen, better strength and power output
  Best time for: high-intensity training, heavy lifts, new PRs
- Luteal phase (days 15-28): Rising progesterone, higher core temperature,
  increased perceived effort, higher fat oxidation
  Best time for: moderate intensity, technique work, endurance

### Estrogen Effects on Training
- Estrogen has anabolic properties — helps muscle protein synthesis
- Provides greater resistance to muscle damage vs men
- Supports collagen synthesis (important for ligament/tendon health)
- Perimenopause/menopause: estrogen decline increases injury risk,
  especially ACL and bone stress fractures

## Strength Training Recommendations

### Volume and Intensity
- Women can tolerate higher training volumes relative to body size
- Similar strength gains to men relative to muscle cross-sectional area
- Benefit from slightly higher rep ranges (10-15) for lower body
- Full body training 3x/week is highly effective for women

### Body Composition
- Women have 6-11% essential body fat (vs 2-5% for men)
- Healthy athletic range: 16-25% body fat
- Avoid excessively low body fat — disrupts hormonal function
- Female Athlete Triad: low energy availability + menstrual dysfunction + low bone density

## Nutrition

### Calorie Needs
- BMR is typically 5-10% lower than men at same body weight
- Use Mifflin-St Jeor with female constant (-161 instead of +5)
- Do not undereat — insufficient energy impairs hormones, recovery, and performance

### Protein
- Same recommendation as men: 1.6-2.2g per kg bodyweight
- Higher protein especially important perimenopausal women to offset muscle loss

### Iron
- Premenopausal women need 18mg/day (vs 8mg for men)
- Athletes lose additional iron through sweat and foot-strike hemolysis
- Low iron = fatigue, poor endurance, impaired cognitive function
- Best sources: red meat, lentils, spinach + vitamin C for absorption

### Calcium and Vitamin D
- Essential for bone density — especially important in women
- Calcium: 1000-1200mg/day
- Vitamin D: 1500-2000 IU/day — most women are deficient

## Common Mistakes
1. Under-eating to stay lean — leads to hormonal disruption
2. Avoiding heavy lifting — fear of "bulking up" (women lack testosterone for this)
3. Only doing cardio — resistance training is more effective for body composition
4. Ignoring menstrual cycle in training programming
5. Not consuming enough iron and calcium
